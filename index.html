Postvention — Definition, Best Practice & Institutional Response

University of Utah Suicide Prevention Task Force · AY 2025–26
This academic year, the University of Utah has experienced student deaths by suicide. The Suicide Prevention Task Force was established in direct response. Postvention is one of three core workgroup areas.

Postvention refers to the organized, compassionate response after a suicide loss — designed to support survivors, reduce contagion risk, stabilize the community, and honor the person who died. Defined by AFSP and SPRC as intervention after a suicide death to promote healing, reduce trauma, and prevent additional deaths among those affected.

Informed best practice (JED Foundation · SPRC · AFSP · American Association of Suicidology): (1) Timely coordinated response within 24–72 hours; (2) Trauma-informed communications following safe messaging guidelines; (3) Proactive sustained outreach to those closely connected to the deceased; (4) Contagion risk monitoring; (5) Structured institutional debrief after each loss.

Our Charge

Why This Task Force Exists
The University of Utah Suicide Prevention Task Force was established in AY 2025–26 in response to student suicide completions on our campus. With no campus-wide suicide prevention or postvention plan in existence, the Task Force was co-founded to build — from the ground up — the institutional infrastructure to prevent, respond to, and recover from suicide across our campus community. This is not a committee. It is a coordinated, Collective Impact initiative with defined workgroups, shared measurement, and an explicit commitment to sustained action over time.

Postvention — Definition & Best Practice

Postvention refers to the organized, compassionate response that occurs after a suicide loss — designed to support survivors, reduce contagion risk, stabilize the community, and honor the person who died. Defined by the American Foundation for Suicide Prevention (AFSP) and Suicide Prevention Resource Center (SPRC) as intervention after a suicide death to promote healing, reduce trauma, and prevent additional deaths among those affected.

Informed best practice (JED Foundation · SPRC · AFSP · American Association of Suicidology): (1) Timely, coordinated institutional response within 24–72 hours; (2) Trauma-informed communications following safe messaging guidelines — no method details, no glorification; (3) Proactive, sustained outreach to those closely connected to the deceased; (4) Contagion risk monitoring in residential and peer communities; (5) Structured institutional debrief and systems learning following each loss.

Prevention Framework — Upstream, Midstream & Downstream

The SH&W division applies a three-tier public health prevention framework across all nine units. Cost-per-dollar figures reflect public health ROI research. Upstream primary prevention costs $0.03 per $1 invested; midstream secondary $0.06–$0.10 per $1; downstream tertiary (clinical treatment) $0.87–$0.91 per $1.

Primary Prevention
Upstream · $0.03/$1
Laws, policies, and structural conditions that support health for all students. Focused on improving fundamental social and economic structures to decrease barriers and improve supports.
Examples: QPR training, peer well-being navigation, health screenings, mindfulness programs, financial education, accessible campus design
Secondary Prevention
Midstream · $0.06–$0.10/$1
Changes at local, community, and organizational levels to change the causes of illness. Focused on improving working and living conditions, promoting healthy behaviors, and limiting illness or injury.
Examples: STI/HIV testing, ATOD screenings, individual counseling, academic accommodations, financial counseling, harm reduction
Tertiary Prevention
Downstream · $0.87–$0.91/$1
Medical interventions, clinical treatments, and intervention of chronic diseases. Focused on providing equitable access to care and services to mitigate the negative impacts of disadvantage on health.
Examples: IOP, psychiatric medication, collegiate recovery, chronic disease management, assistive technology, long-term financial case management

What Is a Logic Model — and Why Are We Using One?

A logic model is a structured visual tool that maps the relationship between a program's resources, activities, outputs, and intended outcomes. It answers a fundamental question: If we invest in these things and take these actions, then what will change — and how will we know? Logic models are a standard tool in public health, education, and program evaluation, widely used by the CDC, NIH, and national foundations to ensure that initiatives are theory-driven, measurable, and accountable over time.

Why the Suicide Prevention Task Force is using a Logic Model: The Task Force was established in response to student deaths by suicide — a moment that demanded both urgency and coordination. A logic model ensures that our response is not simply reactive programming, but a coherent, evidence-informed system with a clear theory of change. It allows us to: (1) align all 60 campus team members around a shared understanding of how our work connects; (2) prioritize activities with the strongest causal link to measurable outcomes; (3) communicate our strategy clearly to institutional leadership, grant reviewers, and community partners; and (4) evaluate whether what we are doing is working — and course-correct when it is not.

The logic model does not represent everything we do — it represents the through-line of our collective impact strategy. Each workgroup's activities are designed to move students across this model: from the conditions we currently have, toward the outcomes our students deserve.

The Task Force Logic Model provides a causal pathway from available resources and activities to measurable student and institutional outcomes. It serves as both a planning tool and an accountability framework — grounding all workgroup activities in a shared theory of change.

Safe Messaging Guidelines — For Faculty, Staff & Peer Leaders
✓ DO
  • · Ask directly: "Are you thinking about suicide?"
  • · Listen without judgment
  • · Stay with the person or connect them to support
  • · Share crisis resources and offer to call together
  • · Follow up after the conversation
  • · Report concerns to UCC or Dean of Students
✗ AVOID
  • · Detailed descriptions of method or location
  • · Presenting suicide as a solution or relief
  • · Sensationalizing or glorifying the loss
  • · "Committed suicide" — use "died by suicide"
  • · Sharing without context or support resources
  • · Leaving a person in crisis alone

Source: American Foundation for Suicide Prevention (AFSP) · Suicide Prevention Resource Center (SPRC) · JED Foundation Safe Messaging Guidelines
